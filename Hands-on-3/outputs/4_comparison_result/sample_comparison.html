Summary

This paper investigates the longitudinal association between social media usage, geographic location (specifically, proximity to climate change impacts), and trait anxiety in adolescents. Following 600 adolescents in California over two years, the study utilized self-report measures for social media usage and trait anxiety (STAI-T), and categorized geographic location based on school districts' exposure to climate-related events. The findings indicate a significant positive correlation between social media usage and trait anxiety, and a main effect of geographic location, with adolescents in more climate-impacted areas exhibiting higher anxiety. While a significant increase in anxiety over time was observed, the hypothesized interaction between social media usage and geographic location was not supported. The authors interpret these findings through an evolutionary lens, emphasizing threat detection and social comparison.

Strengths

* Clear Research Questions and Hypotheses: The paper clearly articulates its research questions and hypotheses, providing a strong framework for the study.
* Longitudinal Design: The two-year longitudinal design is a significant strength, allowing for the investigation of associations over time, which is crucial for understanding developmental phenomena like anxiety. The repeated measures ANOVA showing an increase in anxiety over time is a valuable finding.
* Relevant and Timely Topic: The intersection of social media, climate change, and adolescent mental health is highly relevant and timely, addressing pressing concerns for young people in the digital age.
* Integration of Evolutionary Perspective: The attempt to integrate an evolutionary perspective (threat detection, social comparison) provides a unique theoretical lens for interpreting the findings, moving beyond purely psychological explanations.
* Adequate Sample Size: A sample of 600 adolescents is robust, enhancing the statistical power and generalizability of the findings.
* Standardized Measure: The use of the STAI-T, a well-validated and widely accepted measure of anxiety, strengthens the reliability of the anxiety assessment.

Weaknesses

Limited Depth in Longitudinal Analysis: While the study is longitudinal, the analysis primarily focuses on correlations at Time 2 and a general increase in anxiety. The paper does not fully leverage the longitudinal data to explore how changes in social media usage relate to changes* in anxiety over time, or how geographic location might influence these trajectories.
* Suggestion: Consider employing more sophisticated longitudinal analyses such as cross-lagged panel models or latent growth curve modeling to better understand the temporal dynamics and directional relationships between variables.
* Vague Operationalization of Key Variables: The methods for assessing "Social Media Usage" and "Geographic Location/Climate Impact Score" lack sufficient detail, which hinders replicability and confidence in their validity.
* Suggestion for Social Media Usage: Provide examples of specific questions from the self-developed questionnaire. Detail how "hours per day" was aggregated across platforms. Discuss any efforts to validate this novel measure beyond reporting an alpha coefficient.
* Suggestion for Geographic Location: The "climate impact score" is central to the second hypothesis but is not clearly defined or operationalized in the methods. A dedicated subsection should explain its construction, the specific NOAA data used, the weighting/scoring methodology, and how it differentiates the districts numerically. How was this score used in the ANOVA if the results only refer to "District 1," "District 2," etc.?
* Inconsistent Analytical Approach for Social Media Usage: The results section reports a Pearson correlation for social media usage (implying continuous data) but then a two-way ANOVA with an F-statistic for social media usage (F(1, 597) = 28.56, p < .001), which typically suggests a categorical variable (e.g., high/low usage). This discrepancy needs clarification. If social media usage was treated as a continuous variable, a regression-based approach would be more appropriate for testing main effects and interactions.
* Suggestion: Clarify how social media usage was handled in the ANOVA. If it was dichotomized, explain the rationale and cutoff point. If it was treated as a continuous predictor, a hierarchical multiple regression would be a more suitable analysis, allowing for the inclusion of continuous predictors and interaction terms without loss of information.
Underdeveloped Evolutionary Perspective: While mentioned, the evolutionary perspective feels somewhat superficial. It is introduced in the abstract and discussed briefly, but it doesn't deeply inform the hypotheses or the specific mechanisms being tested. For instance, how would an evolutionary lens predict the moderation* effect of geographic location?
* Suggestion: Integrate the evolutionary framework more thoroughly throughout the introduction, explaining how specific evolutionary mechanisms (e.g., vigilance for local threats, group cohesion, social learning) might lead to the hypothesized relationships and moderation effects.

Detailed Comments

Abstract
* "a location effect was observed" â€“ This could be more precise, e.g., "a significant main effect of geographic location was observed."
* The evolutionary perspective is mentioned but could be slightly more integrated into the specific findings presented in the abstract.

Introduction
* The introduction effectively sets the stage, connecting social media, anxiety, and climate change.
* "Despite these individual findings, a critical gap exists in understanding how geographic location... moderates the relationship..." This clearly articulates the study's unique contribution.
* The research questions and hypotheses are well-stated.

Methods
* Participants: The description of the three school districts (Coastal, Central Valley, Mountain) and their associated climate impacts is good, but linking this more directly to the "climate impact score" is essential.
* Measures:
* Social Media Usage Questionnaire: As noted in weaknesses, more detail is crucial. What platforms were included? How were "hours per day" calculated (e.g., self-estimated, app tracking, average)? What was the range of possible scores?
* Geographic Location: This section is too brief. A dedicated paragraph or subsection is needed to explain:
* The specific criteria used to define "frequency and severity" of climate-related events.
* How the "climate impact score" was calculated (e.g., a composite index, specific metrics).
* The range of possible scores and how the scores of 7.2, 6.5, and 5.8 for the districts were derived.
* Any validation efforts for this score.
* Data Analysis: The description of the ANOVA needs clarification regarding how social media usage (a seemingly continuous variable) was treated. If it was dichotomized, this should be explicitly stated.

Results
* Descriptive Statistics: Providing the mean and SD for trait anxiety and social media usage at both Time 1 and Time 2 would be more informative, especially given the longitudinal design. The climate impact scores are given, but their role in the ANOVA is not explicitly stated in relation to these scores, only district names.
Correlation Analysis: "A significant positive correlation was found between social media usage and trait anxiety at Time 2 (r = .32, p < .001)." Was this correlation also examined at Time 1? And more importantly, was the change in social media usage correlated with the change* in anxiety?
* ANOVA Results:
* The main effect of social media usage (F(1, 597) = 28.56, p < .001) is reported with 1 degree of freedom, strongly suggesting it was treated as a categorical variable (e.g., high vs. low). This needs to be explicitly stated and justified in the Methods.
* The non-significant interaction effect (F(2, 597) = 1.15, p = .32) directly refutes Hypothesis 2. This should be clearly stated.
* The repeated measures ANOVA showing an increase in anxiety from Time 1 to Time 2 (F(1, 598) = 12.78, p < .001) is an important finding and should be highlighted more, perhaps with means for Time 1 and Time 2.

Discussion
* The discussion accurately summarizes the main effects and acknowledges the non-significant interaction.
The evolutionary interpretation is interesting but could be more deeply connected to the specific* findings. For example, how does the lack of a significant interaction effect fit (or not fit) with an evolutionary perspective?
* Limitations: The stated limitations are appropriate. The "cross-sectional nature of the location data" is a good point, but the "climate impact score" could be designed to capture changes if data were available.
* Future Research: The suggestions are solid and logically follow from the limitations and findings.

Questions for Authors

1. Could you provide more specific details on the "self-developed Social Media Usage Questionnaire"? What were the key questions asked, and how was "hours per day" operationalized and calculated across different platforms?
2. Please elaborate on the "climate impact score" for geographic location. How was this score constructed from NOAA data (e.g., specific metrics, weighting, aggregation method)? How does this score numerically differentiate the districts beyond just their names, and how was it incorporated into the ANOVA analysis if the results only refer to "District 1," "District 2," etc.?
3. Regarding the two-way ANOVA, how was "social media usage" treated as a variable? Was it dichotomized (e.g., high vs. low usage), and if so, what was the rationale and cutoff point?
4. Given the longitudinal design, did you analyze the correlation between changes in social media usage and changes in trait anxiety from Time 1 to Time 2? If not, could you comment on why this approach was not taken?
5. Could you discuss potential reasons for the non-significant interaction effect between geographic location and social media usage, especially considering the evolutionary framework? What might this suggest about the interplay of these factors?

Missing References

Based on the provided related work context, the following papers appear highly relevant and could strengthen the paper's background and discussion:

* [1] Topic Shifts as a Proxy for Assessing Politicization in Social Media (arXiv:2312.11326v2) by Locatelli et al.: This paper discusses the politicization of topics like climate change on social media. Given the current paper's focus on climate anxiety and social media, and its evolutionary perspective on threat detection, understanding how climate information is framed and politicized on social media could provide a richer context for the "constant stream of negative news" and its impact on anxiety.
* [2] Invasive species, extreme fire risk, and toxin release under a changing climate (arXiv:2008.01035v1) by Miner et al.: This work directly addresses climate change impacts, specifically extreme fire risk, in Mediterranean ecosystems like California. Citing this could provide stronger scientific backing and specific examples for the "frequency and severity of climate-related events" used to define the geographic locations and their climate impact scores.

Minor Issues

* Abstract: Consider adding the specific F-statistic and p-value for the main effect of social media usage in the abstract for consistency with the location effect.
* Methods - Participants: It would be helpful to state the number of participants from each district, if applicable.
* Results - Correlation Analysis: Specify if the correlation was between Time 2 social media usage and Time 2 trait anxiety, or if it involved Time 1 data.
* Formatting: Ensure consistent capitalization for "State-Trait Anxiety Inventory" (STAI) throughout.

Recommendation

This is a well-conceived and timely study addressing an important area of adolescent mental health. The longitudinal design and the integration of geographic context are significant strengths. However, the paper would benefit substantially from greater methodological detail, particularly regarding the operationalization of social media usage and the climate impact score. Furthermore, a more nuanced analysis of the longitudinal data and a clearer explanation of the analytical approach for social media usage in the ANOVA would enhance its rigor.

I recommend Major Revisions to address the methodological ambiguities, deepen the analytical approach, and more thoroughly integrate the theoretical framework. With these revisions, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the literature on adolescent mental health in the context of social media and climate change.
